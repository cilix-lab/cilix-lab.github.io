The FAA is about to make one of the most consequential technology procurement decisions in its history, and almost nobody outside the aviation trade press is paying attention. I have spent enough years in air traffic management circles to know that is usually how it goes: the infrastructure that moves 2.9 million passengers a day through American airspace runs on systems that most travellers never think about until a ground stop at Newark cascades into missed connections in Denver.

The decision concerns the Strategic Management of Airspace Routing Trajectories system, or SMART. It is an AI-based platform designed to predict airspace conditions months into the future, giving airlines and air navigation service providers something the current system cannot offer: foresight at the strategic planning horizon rather than reactive management hours or minutes before departure. [Flying Magazine reported](https://www.flyingmag.com/faa-smart-ai-air-traffic-system-contract/) that Air Space Intelligence, the company behind the Flyways AI system, is the likely contract recipient, citing multiple people familiar with the selection. An FAA spokesperson told [The Air Current](https://theaircurrent.com/air-traffic-control/faa-selecting-asi-smart-ai-atc-palantir-thales/) to expect an announcement "soon."

This matters for reasons that go well beyond the vendor selection.

## What SMART actually does

Today's air traffic management operates largely in a tactical and pre-tactical window. Controllers manage aircraft in real time. Flow managers adjust sector capacities hours ahead. Airlines file flight plans and adjust for weather. But nobody, not the FAA, not the airlines, not the ANSPs, has a reliable way to look at airspace three months out and say: here is where the constraints will be, here is where demand will exceed capacity, and here is what you should do about it now.

SMART is meant to fill that gap. The system ingests historical traffic data, weather models, airline schedule projections, and infrastructure status to generate predictive airspace pictures at the strategic horizon. An airline planning its summer schedule could, in theory, query SMART and learn that a particular route complex will be capacity-constrained in the second week of July, and adjust its commercial planning accordingly. The FAA could use the same output to preposition resources or reconfigure sector boundaries before the constraint materialises.

This is not an incremental improvement. It is a category shift from reactive to predictive airspace management. And it would be the first operational AI system embedded this deeply in the US air traffic control infrastructure.

## Why ASI, and why now

Air Space Intelligence is not a household name. But its Flyways AI platform already reportedly manages more than 40 percent of US air traffic, according to the company's own claims cited by [The Air Current](https://theaircurrent.com/air-traffic-control/faa-selecting-asi-smart-ai-atc-palantir-thales/) and picked up by [Flying Magazine](https://www.flyingmag.com/faa-smart-ai-air-traffic-system-contract/). Airlines including Alaska Airlines and others have used Flyways to optimise routing and reduce fuel burn. The platform ingests real-time weather, NOTAMs, traffic data, and aircraft performance models to recommend route adjustments that dispatchers can accept or reject. It is, in effect, a production AI system already operating inside the US national airspace, just not yet as FAA infrastructure.

That operational track record gives ASI a narrative edge over the alternatives. Palantir has been working with the FAA on safety analytics and runway incursion monitoring. Thales is a global ATM infrastructure incumbent with deep institutional knowledge. But neither has an AI airspace management platform already handling a material share of live US traffic. If the FAA's selection logic favours proven operational deployment over proposal-stage capability, ASI is the obvious choice.

The timing is not coincidental. The FAA is under enormous pressure.

## The $12.5 billion backdrop

In May 2025, Transportation Secretary Sean Duffy committed to modernising what he called the "antiquated" infrastructure at the core of the national airspace system, much of which dates back to the 1960s. The One Big Beautiful Bill allocated $12.5 billion to the effort, covering over 4,600 sites nationwide. [Forbes detailed the scope](https://www.forbes.com/sites/marisagarcia/2026/05/25/you-can-now-track-faa-efforts-to-fix-antiquated-air-traffic-control/): 5,000 new high-speed network connections, 27,000 new radios, 450 digital voice switches, 612 radars, and a long list of tower and surface surveillance upgrades, all by the end of 2028.

The FAA launched a public-facing [Modern Skies website](https://modernskies.faa.gov/) in late May 2026 to track progress across more than 10,000 individual projects, with data updated monthly. Duffy framed the transparency as a deliberate break from past modernisation efforts that failed, in his words, due to "a lack of transparency."

FAA Administrator Bryan Bedford has been blunt about the state of things. The infrastructure is safe but inefficient, he has said repeatedly in public briefings. Controller staffing remains below target after years of stop-start recruitment disrupted by government shutdowns. [PolitiFact examined](https://www.wlrn.org/business/2026-05-28/did-trumps-faa-slash-the-staffing-target-for-air-traffic-control) whether the Trump administration's FAA had cut controller staffing targets, amid ongoing shortages that have forced the agency to reduce flight volumes at major airports during peak periods.

SMART lands into this context as the intelligence layer on top of the hardware refresh. New radios and fibre connections solve the plumbing problem. SMART addresses the orchestration problem. Both are necessary; neither is sufficient alone.

## The global ATM picture

This is not just an American story. At CANSO's Leadership Summit in late May, the global ATM community reinforced what anyone in the sector already knows: reform is a long game, and the vision of an integrated worldwide system capable of handling traffic growth across borders remains stubbornly distant. [Aviation Week characterised](https://aviationweek.com/air-transport/safety-ops-regulation/daily-memo-why-global-atm-reform-long-game-especially-these) the summit's message as sobering on pace, even as the strategic consensus holds.

Meanwhile, the Civil Aviation Authority of Singapore and the SESAR Joint Undertaking signed a [Joint Declaration](https://aviationweek.com/air-transport/airports-networks/singapore-sesar-aim-improve-europe-asia-traffic-flows) to advance ATM cooperation between Europe and Asia. These are incremental, institutional steps. The US SMART deployment, if it goes live and works as promised, would leapfrog that incrementalism with an operational AI system at national scale.

The rest of the world will be watching. Every ANSP has a version of the same problem: traffic grows, infrastructure ages, controllers are hard to recruit and harder to retain, and AI offers a path that does not require rebuilding the entire system from scratch. If the FAA pulls this off, the SMART model becomes a template for AI-in-ATM adoption globally. If it stumbles, the cautionary tale will cool procurement enthusiasm for years.

## What I am watching for

The contract award itself is the headline, but the operational questions are more interesting. First, how does SMART integrate with the existing ERAM and TBFM infrastructure? The FAA's automation backbone is a complex patchwork, and bolting an AI prediction layer on top of it raises non-trivial data integration and latency questions. Second, what is the human-machine interface? Controllers and traffic managers will not cede authority to a model, nor should they. The system needs to present recommendations in a way that fits existing workflows, not create new cognitive burdens. Third, what does validation look like for a system that predicts months ahead? You cannot run a controlled trial at national scale for something whose value proposition is strategic foresight. The FAA will need a validation framework that is itself novel.

There is also the vendor landscape question. If ASI wins, it establishes a new kind of competitor in the ATM technology market: a Silicon Valley AI company, not a traditional defence or aerospace prime, holding a core FAA infrastructure contract. That would reshape procurement dynamics for years. Palantir's adjacent work on safety analytics suggests the FAA is comfortable with non-traditional vendors, but SMART is a different order of magnitude. Thales, as the incumbent European ATM heavyweight, would see its US ambitions narrowed considerably.

The global ATM community has been talking about trajectory-based operations and predictive airspace management since the SESAR and NextGen programmes were launched. SMART, for all its acronym modesty, could be the first system that actually delivers on those promises at operational scale. The timeline is ambitious. The stakes are proportionate.
